🎯 Investment Thesis

GM remains the cheapest highly-profitable automaker — 5.6x forward earnings, the cohort's best operating margin (9.4%), ~$10B automotive FCF, and a $6B buyback retiring ~8% of the float — and it has been the quiet winner of our coverage (+9% over 3 months, +66% over 12). The picture has stabilized versus early June in two ways: the feared Hormuz-blockade oil spike has eased on a holding US–Iran ceasefire, and RSI has reset from an overbought 80 to a neutral 40. But the core cautions remain: (1) MACD has crossed bearish and the stock was rejected at its $86–88 all-time-high zone; (2) Brent above $100 plus a hawkish Fed under new Chair Warsh keep pressure on the truck franchise (83% of EBIT) and on GM Financial; and (3) the C-suite — Barra (~$39M), Harvey, Jacobson, Hatto — sold ~$50M at $80–85 in late May, and the stock has round-tripped right back to those levels. None of that breaks the valuation case; all of it argues for patience on entry. The new bull optionality (defense/Pentagon re-rate, GM Energy) is real but unmodeled and unproven.

Verdict: Buy-on-weakness with a 12-month base target of $92 (+16%). Preferred entry is the $74–78 zone (SMA200/SMA50) given the bearish MACD and insider overhang; the July 21 Q2 print is the next fundamental checkpoint. Bull case $135 (+70%) requires oil to normalize, the beat-and-raise cycle to survive, and the defense/energy optionality to start showing real revenue.
