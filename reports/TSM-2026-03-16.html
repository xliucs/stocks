Business Overview

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) is the world's largest dedicated independent semiconductor foundry, commanding over 60% of the global foundry market and more than 90% of leading-edge chip production (sub-7nm). The company manufactures chips on behalf of fabless semiconductor companies and IDMs across multiple technology nodes:

  • Advanced Nodes (3nm / 5nm / 7nm) — ~65% of revenue: Powers the most demanding AI, mobile, and HPC applications. Key customers include Apple (iPhone/Mac), Nvidia (AI GPUs), AMD (CPUs/GPUs), Qualcomm, and MediaTek. TSMC's N3E and N3P processes are industry-leading, with 2nm (N2) entering risk production in 2025.
  • Specialty & Mature Nodes (16nm+) — ~35% of revenue: Serves automotive, IoT, industrial, and analog/mixed-signal applications. These nodes provide stable, high-margin revenue with lower capex intensity and multi-year customer commitments.
Competitive Moat: TSMC's moat is arguably the deepest in technology. No competitor can match its yield rates, process technology, and manufacturing scale at leading-edge nodes. Samsung Foundry is the sole alternative at 3nm but trails in yield and customer trust. Intel Foundry Services remains years behind. This monopoly on advanced manufacturing gives TSMC extraordinary pricing power and customer stickiness — switching foundries costs billions and years of re-qualification.

Growth Catalysts

1. AI Chip Demand — The Foundry Bottleneck

TSMC manufactures virtually every AI accelerator in the world: Nvidia's H100/B100/GB200 GPUs, AMD's MI300X, Google's TPU v5, Amazon's Trainium, and Apple's M-series. AI-related revenue is estimated to exceed 20% of total sales in 2026, growing at 50%+ annually. As AI capex from hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon) is projected to exceed $300B in 2026, TSMC is the primary beneficiary as the sole manufacturer capable of producing these chips at scale on 3nm and 5nm nodes.

2. 2nm (N2) Technology Leadership

TSMC's N2 process, based on gate-all-around (GAA) transistor architecture, enters volume production in late 2025/early 2026. N2 offers 10-15% speed improvement and 25-30% power reduction versus N3E. Early customer commitments from Apple, Nvidia, and Qualcomm are strong. N2 will command significant pricing premiums, driving ASP and margin expansion over the next 2-3 years.

3. Global Fab Diversification

TSMC is investing $65B+ in its Arizona fabs (Fab 21), with the first 4nm fab operational in 2025 and a 3nm/2nm fab following by 2028. Additional fabs in Kumamoto, Japan (operational) and Dresden, Germany (under construction) reduce geopolitical risk perception and unlock government subsidies worth $15B+. These investments, while dilutive to near-term margins, secure long-term customer relationships and reduce the Taiwan concentration discount.

4. Advanced Packaging (CoWoS / SoIC)

TSMC's Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate (CoWoS) advanced packaging technology is critical for AI chip performance. Nvidia's B100 and GB200 require CoWoS-L for HBM integration. TSMC is aggressively expanding CoWoS capacity from ~35K wafers/month in 2025 to 80K+ by 2027. This creates an additional high-margin revenue stream and deepens customer lock-in.

5. Next Quarter Earnings Expectations

Consensus expects Q1 2026 revenue of approximately NT$1,117B (~$34.4B USD), representing ~6.8% sequential growth. EPS consensus is $3.30 (ADR), with a range of $3.24 to $3.39. Continued strength in AI and smartphone seasonality recovery should support a beat-and-raise quarter.

Catalyst Watch: Next earnings on 2026-04-16. Consensus expects ~NT$1,117B revenue and $3.30 EPS (ADR). N2 production updates and AI revenue disclosure will be key focus areas. TSMC has beaten consensus in 12 of the last 16 quarters.

Risk Factors

1. Geopolitical Risk — Taiwan Strait Tensions

The existential risk for TSMC investors. Approximately 85% of TSMC's manufacturing capacity remains in Taiwan. A Chinese blockade, invasion, or even sustained military exercises could disrupt global chip supply and crush the stock. While the "silicon shield" theory suggests Taiwan's semiconductor importance deters aggression, this remains an unquantifiable tail risk that warrants a persistent valuation discount.

2. Customer Concentration

Apple alone is estimated to account for ~25% of TSMC's revenue, with Nvidia at ~10-12%, AMD and Qualcomm at ~5-7% each. Losing Apple (which has explored Samsung for select chips) or a major shift in Nvidia's supply chain would materially impact revenue. Hyperscalers developing in-house chips (Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, Microsoft Maia) still use TSMC, but this dynamic bears monitoring.

3. DCF Overvaluation — Base Case Downside

Our base-case DCF at 20.5% FCF growth yields an intrinsic value of ~$220/share, suggesting 35% downside from current levels. Even the bull case at $446 offers only 32% upside. While DCF models struggle to capture TSMC's monopolistic pricing power and strategic premium, the valuation math requires sustained high-teens growth for a decade to justify today's price.

4. Capital Intensity & Margin Pressure from Overseas Fabs

TSMC's annual capex exceeds $30B, with overseas fabs costing 40-50% more than equivalent Taiwan facilities. The Arizona fab has faced cost overruns and labor challenges. As overseas production ramps, blended margins could compress by 200-400bps. Operating cashflow of NT$2.27T is robust, but the gap between operating CF and FCF (NT$643B) reflects heavy reinvestment requirements.

5. Cyclical Demand & Inventory Corrections

Despite AI tailwinds, TSMC remains exposed to smartphone and PC cycles (~50% of revenue). A consumer electronics downturn or AI spending pause could trigger inventory corrections and utilization rate declines at mature and mid-range nodes. Beta of 1.28 confirms above-market sensitivity to macro conditions.
