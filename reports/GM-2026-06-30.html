🎯 Investment Thesis

GM remains the cheapest highly-profitable automaker — 5.5x forward earnings, the cohort's best operating margin (9.4%), ~$10B automotive FCF, and a $6B buyback retiring ~8% of the float — and it is still up +58% over twelve months even after this month's pullback. Two things have shifted versus the June-21 read: the chart has deteriorated (GM lost its 50-day, MACD is bearish, and it now sits at $77.08 testing the SMA200 at $74.74), while the macro has turned more hawkish (Warsh non-committal on hikes, BofA calling three). The core cautions from prior reports persist and have partly played out: (1) the C-suite — Barra (~$39M), Harvey, Jacobson, Hatto — sold ~$50M at $80–85 in late May, and the stock has now fallen through those levels; (2) higher-for-longer rates pressure the truck franchise (83% of EBIT) and GM Financial; and (3) June brought split analyst revisions (Citi $131 vs Goldman/Barclays/Piper/RBC trims). The new optionality is factory automation/robotics (Factory Zero cobots, rumored $NVDA tie-up) — real, but unmodeled and labor-contentious. None of this breaks the valuation case; DCF base ($93), consensus PT ($95), and base-case P/E ($92) all cluster near $92, ~19% above spot.

Verdict: Buy-on-weakness with a 12-month base target of $92 (+19%). The pullback to the SMA200 zone ($74–77) is a more attractive entry than the ATH chase; the July 21 Q2 print is the next fundamental checkpoint. Bull case $135 (+75%) requires the beat-and-raise cycle to survive, EV losses to keep shrinking, and the automation/defense optionality to show real revenue. Size for a $54 bear tail in a hike-and-recession scenario.
